Forest Resources

Wood and Timber Products & Non-Timber Forest Products (NTFPs)

Construction Material: Used in building homes, furniture, and infrastructure. Pulp and Paper: Essential for producing paper, cardboard, and packaging. Fuelwood and Charcoal: A primary energy source in many rural areas. Resins and Latex: Used in rubber production, varnishes, and adhesives. Bamboo and Rattan: Harvested for handicrafts, furniture, construction, and as a sustainable alternative to plastic. Medicinal Plants: Many plants like quinine, aloe, and ginseng are used in traditional and modern medicine. Fruits, Nuts, and Seeds: Examples include acai berries, Brazil nuts, pine nuts, and wild berries.

Forest Resources

Edible Resources, Fibers and Textiles & Oils and Essential Oils

Wild Fruits and Berries: Such as mangoes, berries, and figs. Honey and Beeswax: Collected from forest bees and used in food and skincare. Mushrooms and Truffles: Some of which are highly valuable, like morels and chanterelles. Game Meat: Such as deer and wild boar, which is hunted sustainably in some regions. Plant Fibers: Like hemp, jute, and flax, used for ropes, cloth, and paper. Silk: Produced by silkworms, often raised in or around forest areas. Palm Oil and Coconut Oil: Widely used in cooking and cosmetics. Essential Oils: Such as eucalyptus, cedarwood, and sandalwood, used in aromatherapy, skincare, and health products.

Forest Resources

Gums and Saps & Environmental Services

Gum Arabic and Frankincense: Used in food, pharmaceuticals, and cosmetics. Maple Syrup: Produced from the sap of maple trees, especially in North America. Carbon Sequestration: Forests capture and store carbon, mitigating climate change. Water Filtration and Retention: Forests help maintain clean water supplies by filtering pollutants and regulating groundwater. Biodiversity Habitat: Forests provide homes for countless species of flora and fauna, maintaining ecological balance.
